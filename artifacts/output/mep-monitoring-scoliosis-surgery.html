Why MEP monitoring is essential in scoliosis correction

Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) and adult degenerative scoliosis corrections rank among the most neurologically demanding procedures performed in the OR. The surgical steps — pedicle screw placement, rod insertion, curve correction maneuvers — each carry real risk to the motor pathways running through the anterior spinal cord. A motor deficit that goes undetected can be irreversible. That is the exact problem MEP monitoring exists to solve.

Motor evoked potentials work by delivering a transcranial electrical stimulus that depolarizes the motor cortex, generating a descending volley through the corticospinal tract to the target muscle. The resulting compound muscle action potential (CMAP) is recorded from needle electrodes placed in bilateral muscle groups — typically the tibialis anterior, abductor hallucis, vastus medialis, and upper extremity muscles depending on the level of surgical risk. Unlike SSEPs, which monitor the sensory dorsal columns, MEPs provide a direct window into the motor tract — the pathway most at risk during deformity correction.

For IONM techs new to scoliosis cases, the most important conceptual shift is understanding that you are watching a dynamic, actively changing signal throughout the entire case — not a stable baseline you periodically compare. Deformity correction applies real mechanical forces to the cord, and changes can be abrupt.

Establishing a reliable MEP baseline and recognizing significant changes

A strong, reproducible baseline is the foundation of every meaningful MEP alert. Before the first incision, you must achieve stable, repeatable CMAPs in all monitored muscle groups. This means:

  • Anesthetic stability: MEPs are profoundly sensitive to anesthetic depth. Total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA) with propofol and remifentanil is the standard for MEP-compatible cases — volatile agents above 0.5 MAC will suppress or abolish cortical MEPs. Confirm TIVA is running and anesthetic levels are stable before recording your baseline.
  • Neuromuscular blockade (NMB): Muscle relaxants block the neuromuscular junction and eliminate the CMAP. Ensure NMB has fully reversed (train-of-four ratio ≥ 0.9) before recording. Re-confirm throughout the case if additional relaxant is given.
  • Stimulation parameters: Transcranial MEP stimulation uses a train of 4–7 pulses, typically 50–75 ms interstimulus interval, at intensities ranging from 100–400 mA. Start at the lowest effective intensity that yields reliable, consistent responses and document those parameters as your standard for the case.
  • Bilateral symmetry: Document left and right amplitudes separately. Asymmetry at baseline can be normal — what matters is change from that individual patient's baseline, not comparison to the other side.
⚠ Standard MEP Alert Criteria

A ≥50% reduction in peak-to-peak CMAP amplitude compared to baseline, sustained across two consecutive stimulations, constitutes a significant MEP alert requiring immediate notification of the surgical and anesthesia teams.

Some institutions also use complete signal loss (amplitude falling to noise floor) as an immediate stop-and-assess threshold, regardless of the 50% rule. Know your institution's specific criteria before the case begins and confirm them with the attending neurophysiologist.

When MEP alerts fire: communication, intervention, and documentation

An MEP alert is a team event — not just a tech event. When a significant amplitude change occurs, the response must be fast, systematic, and clearly communicated. Here is the standard sequence:

  1. Troubleshoot first, but fast. Before alarming the surgeon, take 15–30 seconds to rule out technical causes: check stimulation impedances, confirm NMB status with the anesthesiologist, verify electrode positions haven't shifted, and run one additional stimulation trial. Do not delay if the second run confirms the change — move immediately to step 2.
  2. Notify the surgeon and anesthesiologist simultaneously. Use a direct, unambiguous statement: "I have a 60% MEP amplitude decrease in bilateral tibialis anterior, confirmed on two runs." Avoid hedging language. Surgeons in the middle of a correction maneuver need a clear signal.
  3. Support the intervention. The team will typically pause surgical manipulation, ask anesthesia to optimize MAP (mean arterial pressure), check implant positions, and possibly reverse recent correction steps. Your job during this period is continuous monitoring — run MEP stimulations every 1–2 minutes and report real-time changes in amplitude recovery or further decline.
  4. Document everything with timestamps. Record the exact time of amplitude change, the parameters at alert, the intervention(s) performed, and the amplitude trajectory through recovery. This documentation is clinically essential and legally significant.

If MEP signals do not recover despite interventions — particularly if bilateral lower extremity responses are lost completely — the surgeon may elect to perform a wake-up test (Stagnara test), waking the patient sufficiently to assess voluntary motor function before proceeding. This is a high-intensity moment that requires calm, clear communication from the IONM team.

MEP monitoring in scoliosis is not a passive recording exercise. It demands active vigilance, technical precision, and the clinical communication skills to act decisively when the nervous system signals danger. Building these competencies early — and having the documentation infrastructure to support them — is what separates a reactive IONM team from a proactive one.
